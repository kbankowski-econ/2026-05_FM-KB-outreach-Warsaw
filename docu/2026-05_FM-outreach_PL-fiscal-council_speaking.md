Slide 1
*Welcome and brief overview of what the next 30 minutes will cover.*

Slide 2
- We finalized this report in a turbulent moment. After 2025's **record-high policy uncertainty** had started to ease <span style="color:royalblue">*(left panel)*</span>, the **Middle East war broke out in late February** and reshaped much of our analysis.
- First question on everyone's mind: how large is the fiscal impact? Hard to size precisely — depends on duration and scope.
- But history is clear: energy price shocks have **deeply asymmetric effects** <span style="color:royalblue">*(right panel)*</span>. Importers, especially low-income ones, take the hit first.
- Exporter gains exist but are limited: fewer beneficiaries, much of the windfall is saved, and this conflict directly involves **Gulf producers whose own export channels are disrupted**.
- Net globally: very few clear winners.

Slide 3
- Our baseline assumes the war is **short-lived**, with production impediments gone by mid-year. Modest direct fiscal effect.
- Two adverse scenarios — one more severe — sit in the risk section.
- Even on this benign baseline, the picture isn't pretty: global growth around **3 percent** <span style="color:royalblue">*(left panel)*</span>, deficit stuck near **5 percent of GDP** <span style="color:royalblue">*(middle panel)*</span>, and **global debt hits 100 percent of GDP in 2029** <span style="color:royalblue">*(right panel)*</span>.
- That debt level was last reached only in the **aftermath of World War II**.
- The deeper signal: 2025 growth surprised on the upside (around 3.4 percent), yet the fiscal outlook still deteriorated.
- **Polish growth** last year even marginally exceeded the global pace, yet the deficit hit a **record high**. It is set to narrow over the coming years, but **from a very high base** — leaving the **debt ratio on a steep upward path**, projected to reach about **78 percent of GDP by 2031**, up from roughly 53 percent in 2021.

Slide 4
- Behind the global fiscal deterioration there is one defining force: **rising interest expenditures** <span style="color:royalblue">*(left panel)*</span>.
- Up from **roughly 2 percent of GDP four years ago to nearly 3 percent last year** — and projected to keep rising.
- The **primary balance** is improving slightly <span style="color:royalblue">*(right panel)*</span> — but those gains are being **consumed** by the growing interest bill.
- **This rising-interest-bill dynamic is particularly pronounced in Poland**: interest payments stood at just **1 percent of GDP four years ago** and have climbed quickly to **2.5 percent last year**, as debt grows and higher yields feed through.
- And judging by earlier forecast vintages, **the scale of this increase was hardly foreseeable**.

Slide 5
- The surge in the interest bill is a direct reflection of **high interest rates**.
- Rates have been at **multi-year highs since 2022** <span style="color:royalblue">*(left panel)*</span>. As governments refinance long-term debt, those higher marginal rates keep feeding into effective costs.
- Outside Japan, the rise had begun to level off — until the war. **Long-end yields jumped**: UK by 50–60 basis points.
- Financing pressures are set to intensify.
- One subtlety <span style="color:royalblue">*(right panel)*</span>: term spreads have narrowed since the war — **but for the wrong reason**. It's a **short-end spike** reflecting near-term inflation fears, not a long-end improvement.

Slide 6
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel)*</span>: despite high financing costs, the **interest-growth differential remains favorable** at the global level. But the margin is **too narrow** to offset **persistent primary deficits** — and that's before even factoring in the **positive stock-flow adjustment**.
- This tendency is **even more visible for Poland**: its **automatic debt dynamics are still clearly favorable** — growth and inflation continue to outpace the effective interest rate — yet **nowhere near enough to offset the sizable primary deficits** in prospect.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: most of the projected rise in global debt comes from the **two largest economies — China and the US**.
- The counterweight is modest: **Japan** stands out — debt projected to fall by about **14 percentage points** thanks to **strong nominal growth** — but far from enough to offset the broader upward trajectory.

Slide 7
- The global figures we just looked at **mask substantial heterogeneity** across regions and countries.
- A recurring theme today: this landscape is **dominated by China and the US**.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel)*</span>: both run **enormous deficits today** and are **projected to keep doing so**.
- **Poland's deficit** has recently been close to what we see in the two largest economies — but here it looks more like a **temporary** feature: it is **projected to narrow** over the horizon.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: the two largest economies also accounted for the **lion's share** of recent global debt accumulation — and that pattern continues going forward.

Slide 8
- Beyond the level itself, the Fiscal Monitor flags two structural concerns.
- First <span style="color:royalblue">*(left panel)*</span>: the **holder structure of debt is changing**. Until recently, domestic official sectors absorbed most of the increase — central-bank QE purchases (orange bars). With monetary policy normalizing, that buyer is stepping back. Future debt has to be absorbed by **price-sensitive investors** — non-banks, hedge funds — who are far more **prone to sentiment shifts**.
- Second <span style="color:royalblue">*(right panel)*</span>: some countries — **especially the US** — have been issuing at **shorter maturities**. A pandemic-era flexibility move that stuck. Result: more frequent rollover, and the **convenience yield** that Treasuries usually enjoy is now markedly higher.

Slide 9
- We've laid out the **global picture**, with some Polish comparisons given the venue.
- The rest of the presentation delves into the **regional and country-level outlook**. But first, a brief detour worth taking.
- Much of today's debate is fixated on the **conjunctural** — mainly the Middle East war and its repercussions. **That matters — but it shouldn't obscure something more fundamental.**
- Fiscal policy today is in a **markedly weaker structural position than a decade ago**. That's the lens for the next four slides.

Slide 10
- We did a small exercise comparing the average public finance position over **2024–29** to the corresponding period a decade ago. Both windows are **relatively free of cyclical factors**, so they give us a fair read on the structural picture.
- The charts plot **cumulative distributions across all countries** for the main fiscal variables.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel)*</span>: the **median country's primary balance is unchanged**. But the **GDP-weighted average has deteriorated massively** — from −1 to worse than −2 percent of GDP — pointing to the role of the major economies. **For Poland the shift is striking**: from near balance to a **sizable deficit**, sitting in the **left tail** of the distribution.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Middle panel)*</span>: **interest payments are worse across the board** — no exceptions.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: public **debt is consistently higher**, especially on the weighted average, which rose by close to **20 percentage points of GDP**. **Poland's increase is equally notable**, shifting the country toward the **high-debt end** of the distribution.

Slide 11
- One way to summarize the state of public finances: the **fiscal gap** — observed primary balance minus the level needed to stabilize debt.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel)*</span>: a decade ago this gap was **clearly positive**. Today it has **deteriorated by more than 1 percentage point** — close to zero.
- The **snowball effect contributes essentially nothing**: high interest costs are offset by higher inflation and slightly better growth.
- The damage comes from **wider primary deficits** — countries collect less and spend significantly more.
- Caveat: this metric **excludes stock-flow adjustments**. With SFA included the starting point would already be negative, and the deterioration even larger.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: country contributions. **China and the US dominate**. **Germany** is also a notable contributor.

Slide 12
- Within the global picture, **national trajectories diverge sharply**.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel)*</span> — **Germany**: a **pronounced reversal** — the fiscal gap shifts from **+3.6 to −1.5 percent of GDP**. Almost entirely driven by **higher primary spending** following the **debt-brake reform**, which is financing public investment and other national priorities.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Middle panel)*</span> — **Japan**: the fiscal gap **rises to 4 percent of GDP** from around zero a decade ago — a notable **improvement**, supported by **stronger revenues** and **markedly higher inflation**. The underlying position, though, remains **challenging**.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span> — **Poland** sits closer to **Germany**: a **comparable deterioration in the fiscal gap**, and — as in Germany — one **driven mainly by higher primary spending**.

Slide 13
- This slide zooms in on **Poland** and the drivers of its debt dynamics. It may look busy at first glance, but the message is **simple**.
- Across **most of the variables that drive debt dynamics**, Poland's average for **2024–29** marks a **structural deterioration** versus its own **pre-pandemic (2014–19) norm**. The only **offsetting factors** are a **higher revenue ratio** and **stronger price dynamics**.
- The rest have deteriorated markedly — either **systematically across all countries**, as with the effective interest rate, or **specifically for Poland**, worsening its position relative to peers — most notably on **primary spending**.

Slide 14
- The rest of this section walks through the fiscal outlook **by country group**.
- Standard split: **advanced economies, emerging markets, low-income countries**.
- One exception: given how dominant **China and the US** are, we **pull them out** and treat them separately — US out of AEs, China out of EMs.
- In the interest of time, I will focus on the **narratives most relevant for Poland** and keep the rest brief.

Slide 15
- The US runs **very high deficits — around 7 percent of GDP** — with no consolidation in sight, and debt is on track to reach **140 percent of GDP** by the end of our horizon.
- Two recent developments cut in opposite directions: a **tariff-driven revenue boost** of about 1 percent of GDP, now clouded by the **February Supreme Court ruling**; and the **One Big Beautiful Bill**, which locks in **lower revenue** over the long run.

Slide 16
- China runs **expansionary fiscal policy** alongside **sharply rising debt**, lifted further by a landmark **debt-swap program** bringing local-government-financing-vehicle liabilities onto the sovereign books.
- Worth flagging the **policy-prescription asymmetry** with the US: both run large primary deficits despite no negative output gap, yet the recommendation is to **tighten in the US** and **maintain a supportive stance in China** to facilitate the business model transition.

Slide 17
- Advanced economies excluding the US are the **only region** where aggregate debt is **lower than pre-pandemic** and deficits are the **smallest** — though still material. Within the group, **Japan** stands out: its debt ratio is projected to fall significantly, driven entirely by **favorable automatic debt dynamics**.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: **defense spending in Europe** — directly relevant for Poland as one of the **16 countries** that activated **national escape clauses**. These were **temporary by design**, but our prior FM analysis showed defense is one of the **most rigid spending categories** — wages, long-term commitments, technology servicing. The tension: the spending is likely to **stick**, while the framework was meant to be temporary.

Slide 18
- For emerging markets, overall balances are **improving** but still large, and debt is likely to keep rising. The striking story of the year: **extremely favorable financing conditions in 2025** — record issuance alongside a major **compression of spreads**.
- **Poland benefited too**, riding the same wave of compressed spreads and easy market access. But our analytical work shows the compression traces **mostly to global factors** — financial conditions, equity markets, **dollar depreciation** — and those have already begun to unwind since the war, with spreads widening sharply, especially for **low-rated** issuers. The take-away: **2025's compression cannot be taken for granted**.

Slide 19
- For **low-income countries**, low debt ratios mask **severe constraints**: rising interest burdens — close to **a third of revenue** in some cases — and **declining official aid**, concentrated on the African continent.

Slide 20
- Having walked through the **detailed outlook**, we now turn to the **risks surrounding it** — what they are, and **where we can quantify them**.
- The risk section also pulls in **several analytical exercises** that directly inform the outlook.

Slide 21
- Risks remain **heavily tilted to the downside**.
- Our **debt-at-risk** measure — the 95th percentile of the projected debt distribution three years out — now sits at **117.2 percent of GDP for 2028** <span style="color:royalblue">*(left panel)*</span>.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Middle panel)*</span>: a **severe Middle East scenario**. A prolonged conflict that lowers global GDP by 2.3 percent and lifts inflation by 2.6 pp by 2027 raises debt-at-risk by **4.0 pp of GDP** — and the hit is **highly asymmetric**: **EMDEs +6.2 pp**, AEs only +2.8 pp.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: AI is **dual-natured**. Faster productivity gains would *improve* debt dynamics — but high equity concentrations and debt-financed AI investment make a **disorderly correction** a real risk. A **20 percent fall in US equities** (about half the dot-com drop), with global spillovers, raises debt-at-risk by another **2.4 pp**.

Slide 22
- Meanwhile, the **scale of Treasury issuance is eroding their convenience yield** — the safety and liquidity premium they have traditionally enjoyed.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel)*</span>: the **AAA corporate–Treasury spread has compressed** — domestic erosion.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: the **international convenience yield versus G10 peers has gone negative**. Treasuries now yield **more** than hedged G10 sovereigns in synthetic-dollar terms.
- As the US safety premium falls, the **global risk-free benchmark effectively rises** — lifting sovereign borrowing costs **worldwide**.

Slide 23
- Issuing short-term **lowers the interest bill** when the yield curve slopes up — but it brings **clear vulnerabilities**.
- We built a **model** to quantify this.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel)*</span>: under a financing-conditions tightening, a higher share of short-term debt **lifts the debt-to-GDP ratio** (baseline bars). If investors also start questioning **rollover capacity** — demanding higher yields or pulling out of auctions — the effect is **substantially larger** (heightened-rollover bars).
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: each of these channels is **magnified when starting debt is already high**.

Slide 24
- The shifts I just described **have implications for global spillovers**.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel)*</span>: IMF staff analysis of an **expansionary Treasury supply shock** — issuance above what investors expected. A **1 bp rise in US yields passes through 0.8 bp** into foreign 10-year yields.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel)*</span>: the real effect — foreign **industrial production falls by about 0.5 percent after one year** — a mix of financial spillovers and weaker US demand.
- Take-away: **US fiscal stress is not a US-only problem.**

Slide 25
- Beyond cyclical and acute risks, **slow-moving structural vulnerabilities** remain a persistent source of fiscal risk — and they **differ sharply by income group**. To map them, we ran the **full corpus of recent IMF country staff reports through large language models**, extracting every vulnerability cited and tallying their frequency by group.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Left panel — advanced economies)*</span>: **demographic pressure dominates** — aging-related spending shows up as a fiscal risk in **63 percent of country reports**. Healthy aging may offer some offsets, but **longer lifespans raise lifetime costs**, especially through expensive end-of-life care.
- <span style="color:royalblue">*(Right panel — emerging markets and developing economies)*</span>: a very different landscape — **SOE contingent liabilities top the list at 68 percent**, followed by **natural disasters (52 percent)** and **banking-sector vulnerabilities (47 percent)**.

Slide 26
- We've now walked through **where public finances stand** and the **risks ahead**.
- A demanding environment **sharpens** rather than dulls the obligation to offer **concrete guidance** — and the next slides set out the **policy recommendations** we see as best suited to this moment.

Slide 27
- **Keep crisis support targeted and temporary.** Where fiscal space exists, use **existing transfers** and short-term liquidity for viable firms — not costly broad-based measures. **Align with monetary policy** to avoid prolonging inflation. Let automatic stabilizers work; where space is tight, **reprioritize within the budget** rather than adding to the deficit. Commodity exporters: **resist procyclical spending**, channel windfalls into buffers or sovereign wealth funds.
- **Make fiscal policy more forward-looking.** In a shock-prone environment, **well-designed medium-term adjustment plans** are now urgent.
- **Credibility now hinges on transparency and communication.** Hidden deficits, vague promises, and opaque contingent liabilities corrode trust. Public debt perceptions often diverge sharply from reality.
- **Credible medium-term debt-management strategies** — aligned with fiscal frameworks and market conditions — help manage rollover risk and lower borrowing costs.

Slide 28
- **United States**: the arithmetic is inescapable — stabilizing the debt path requires action on **both revenue and expenditure**, including the **major entitlement programs**.
- **Other advanced economies with large debt loads**: concrete, well-sequenced **consolidation measures**. European governments must reconcile **defense commitments with aging-related pressures** through tangible spending reprioritization.
- **Poland**: needs to **preserve its record of impressive macroeconomic stability**. We recognize the **large defense-related spending needs** — but the **deficit reflects more than defense**: of the **5.6 pp rise since 2021**, only **2.1 pp came from defense**. This is opening a clear **vulnerability**. Safeguarding fiscal sustainability at current spending levels would require **gradually rising public revenues**.
- **China**: near-term **fiscal expansion focused on consumption and the property sector** should continue until reflation is durable. Over time: significant **consolidation**, **tax and social-security reform**, and **restructuring of LGFV debt**.
- **Emerging markets**: address **contingent liabilities**, **phase out fuel subsidies**, **broaden the tax base**.
- **Low-income countries**: with **declining external aid**, **domestic revenue mobilization** is essential. Well-executed **tax-administration reforms** can deliver meaningful gains.

Slide 31
- A closer look at Poland's centerpiece fiscal anchor — the **Stabilizing Expenditure Rule (SER)**. The slide compares the current rule (**v1.0**) with a proposed **upgraded, EU-aligned design (v2.0)** across **five dimensions**. Headline first: the SER **worked** — it anchored fiscal restraint and helped put debt on a **downward path before the pandemic** — but its design now shows clear limits.
- **Time Horizon.** Today only a **1-year binding limit** — too short for credible medium-term planning. Upgrade: a **multi-year binding limit** on a **4-to-7-year horizon**, aligned with the reformed EU framework.
- **Growth Formula.** Largely **backward-looking** (a t−6 to t+1 average) — misses slowing medium-term growth and structural headwinds. Upgrade: **forward-looking growth indicators**, a 3-year projection with an **error-correction mechanism**, and switching to the **GDP deflator** for more stable price adjustment.
- **Coverage.** Only about **70 percent** of general government is bound, with **off-budget leaks** — for instance transfers to **BGK**. Upgrade: full **ESA 2010 alignment**, bringing in **extrabudgetary funds and securities transfers** to stop circumvention.
- **Oversight.** Today only an **ex-post legal audit by NIK** — no independent check on the macro-fiscal projections feeding the formula. Upgrade: **ex-ante and ex-post validation by an independent fiscal council** — a path the new council now opens up.
- **Shock Response.** Today, **ad-hoc amendments** and a narrow **epidemic-only escape trigger**. Upgrade: a **standalone severe-slowdown trigger** with a **debt-linked return trajectory** — flexible in the bad times, disciplined on the way back.

Slide 32
- An **independent fiscal council** was a missing piece to make the spending rule credible. Five core functions.
- **Macro-fiscal evaluation**: a mandate to scrutinize the **quality and realism of the projections** underpinning the **Medium-Term Fiscal Framework** and the **SER formula**. Optimistic forecasts are how spending rules quietly fail — an independent check guards against that.
- **Escape-clause stewardship**: monitor the **activation and extension** of escape clauses, **cost the extraordinary measures** they permit, and police the **return path** back to the rule limits — so temporary deviations don't become permanent.
- **Rigorous compliance oversight**: detailed **ex-ante and ex-post** assessments of adherence to the SER. This **bridges the current gap** — today there is no independent body doing either the prospective or the retrospective analysis.
- **Sustainability safeguards**: regular **debt sustainability analyses** to keep national policy consistent with **long-term objectives** and the **new EU framework**.
- **Institutional autonomy**: none of this works without **full operational independence** and **adequate resources** — the council must be the authoritative **"single voice"** on fiscal transparency and discipline.
